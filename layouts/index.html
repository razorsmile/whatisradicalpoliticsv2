Why This Course?

In response to growing interest in radical politics, this course provides critical thinking tools rather than campaign training or argumentation techniques.

This course focuses on understanding multiple radical traditions, developing analytical tools, grounding theory in local contexts, and building collective knowledge through co-research.

Course Philosophy

Experience-Based: Participants are co-educators. We draw on existing knowledge and lived experience.

Locally Grounded: Sessions connect to your community's specific history, present conditions, and possibilities.

Practically Oriented: We create tangible resources like counter-power maps and research networks.

Using These Materials

All materials are freely available to copy, share, and adapt for non-commercial political education.

  • Facilitators: Adapt materials to your local context and share improvements
  • Participants: Form study groups and apply tools in your organizing practice
  • Organizations: Run the course in your community and create local versions

Course Overview

This course introduces participants to radical political thinking through four academic lenses: Philosophy, Political Theory, History, and Sociology. Each session provides analytical tools for understanding radical politics while maintaining a focus on local contexts. The course is designed to be accessible to those without prior academic background, emphasizing practical application and local relevance.

The idea for the course is that it is a tool for outreach. It is designed to be used to create a space for facilitated conversation in a convivial setting. Imagine doing a listening exercise in a local community where you're introducing yourselves as local organisers and aiming to develop some community organising work. This course could be used as a 'next step' to offer people on the doorstep when you've finished your questions. Or it could be run as a local group activity that can be offered to a street or neighbourhood. Or it can be run as a way to promote healthy political discussion within a small group of party activists. It's a tool, and if it has a use you will find it.

The other goal of the course is to offer a bit of an introduction to the 'language of the left' that can help people engage with radical ideas and discussions. Often the left uses a bunch of jargon and a set of ideas that many working class people can find alienating and strange. The aim here is to offer some space in which new comrades encounter and learn some of this language, in a relaxed and conversational way.

It also emphasises the plurality of radical politics in order to try and move away from the potential disappointment and disillusionment that can come from encountering sometimes heated debates within the left. The goal is to suggest that disagreement and difference are not to be a source of division but a source of strength and learning.

For this to work the facilitator must embrace the course philosophy and approach that is centred on the reality of people's lives and experiences. The ideas and concepts need to be connected to lived experience and local struggles, enabling participants to teach each other what the concepts means through showing how they fit - or not - with their own experiences of bosses, landlords, cops and the state in their actual life. The sharing of these experiences with each other is perhaps the most valuable thing the course can produce.

This is co-learning, not teaching. At the end of the course your aim as a facilitator should be that at least one of the participants can go on to facilitate the course themselves in another run. Each one, teach one.

Target Audience

Left-wing activists and working-class people interested in or involved with Your Party UK, with no assumed prior academic background.

Course Structure

Duration: 4 sessions
Session Length: 2 hours
Approach: Critical thinking focused

Key Features

Jargon Buster • Experiential Learning • Local Focus • Optional Deeper Engagement • Housing as Running Example

Four Week Journey

WEEK 1

Radical Political Traditions

Philosophy Lens

What makes politics 'radical' and what traditions inform radical thinking?

WEEK 2

In, Against, and Beyond the State

Political Theory Lens

How do different radical traditions relate to state power?

WEEK 3

Radicals in our town - Past and Present

History Lens

What can we learn from our local radical history?

WEEK 4

Mapping Working-Class Counter-Power

Sociology Lens

Where does working-class power exist in our town or city today?

Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, participants will have an introduction to:

  1. Multiple radical political traditions and their tensions
  2. Different relationships between radical movements and the state
  3. Skills in local radical history research
  4. Mapping local working-class counter-power and mutual aid networks
  5. Applying critical thinking to contemporary political questions
