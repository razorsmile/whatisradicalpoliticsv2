Week 2 Facilitator Script

This is a script for the second week of the Radical Education course. It is designed to help facilitators run the session effectively.

HOUR 1: Structured Content (60 mins)

Opening & Recap (10 mins)

Facilitator Script:

"Welcome back. Last week we explored six radical traditions. Tonight we're examining how different traditions relate to state power - arguably THE central strategic question for radical politics.

Who brought research to share? We'll hear those in hour two.

First, let's briefly check in: what stayed with you from last week?"

[2-3 brief shares]

Part 1: Three Orientations to the State (20 mins)

Facilitator Script:

"Tonight's framework comes from a phrase used by various movements: 'In, Against, and Beyond the State.' These aren't mutually exclusive strategies - many movements combine them."

[Draw triangle on flipchart with three points labeled]

IN THE STATE
Working through electoral politics, policy, and state institutions to achieve radical goals.

  • Logic: The state has unique power to redistribute resources and reshape society
  • Methods: Elections, legislation, public ownership, welfare systems
  • Brighton examples: Your Party councilors, Greens implementing community wealth building
  • Risks: Co-optation, bureaucratization, limits of parliamentary democracy

AGAINST THE STATE
Confronting state power through resistance, disruption, and refusal.

  • Logic: The state is fundamentally an instrument of domination that cannot be reformed
  • Methods: Direct action, strikes, blockades, squatting, civil disobedience
  • Brighton examples: Anti-raids networks, squatting movements, poll tax resistance
  • Risks: Repression, marginalization, inability to build alternatives

BEYOND THE STATE
Building parallel institutions and counter-power outside state control.

  • Logic: We must create new forms of social organization rather than seizing existing ones
  • Methods: Cooperatives, mutual aid networks, community land trusts, alternative currencies
  • Brighton examples: Brighton Food Co-op, community gardens, time banks
  • Risks: Isolation, lack of resources, limited scale

These orientations reflect different theories of power and change."

Part 2: The Housing Crisis as Case Study (20 mins)

Facilitator Script:

"Let's apply this framework to Brighton's housing crisis. Some context:

  • Average house price: £525,000 (15x average salary)
  • Average rent: £1,800/month for 2-bed
  • 2,000+ on council housing waiting list
  • 150+ rough sleepers

How might each orientation address this?

IN THE STATE - Your Party's Housing Platform:

  • Rent controls and tenant protections
  • Mass council housing construction
  • Community right to buy
  • Wealth taxes to fund social housing

This assumes we can win electoral power and use the state to decommodify housing.

AGAINST THE STATE - Direct Action Approaches:

  • Rent strikes and eviction resistance
  • Squatting empty properties
  • Blocking estate agent offices
  • Disrupting property developer meetings

The Autonomous Winter Shelter in Brighton operated on these principles - occupying empty buildings to house rough sleepers despite legal threats.

BEYOND THE STATE - Alternative Models:

  • Community Land Trusts (like Brighton's Bunker Housing Co-op)
  • Housing cooperatives
  • Mutual aid networks for housing support
  • Self-build projects

These create non-commodified housing outside market and state systems.

Most effective movements combine strategies. The 1915 Glasgow Rent Strikes used 'against' tactics (strikes) to win 'in' victories (rent controls) while building 'beyond' structures (tenants' organizations)."

Part 3: Strategic Debates (10 mins)

Facilitator Script:

"These orientations raise crucial debates:

The Parliamentary Road?
Can we vote our way to socialism? Your Party says yes with the right movement behind it. Critics point to Chile's Allende, Greece's Syriza - radical governments crushed or co-opted.

Diversity of Tactics?
Should movements combine all three orientations or does that dilute focus? Some argue for 'dual power' - building counter-institutions while also engaging electoral politics.

Prefiguration vs Power?
Do we focus on modeling the future society now (beyond) or seizing levers of change (in)? Do means determine ends?

Local vs National?
Brighton City Council has limited powers. Can municipal socialism work within national constraints? Or must change happen at every scale simultaneously?"

HOUR 2: Open Discussion (60 mins)

Jargon Buster (5 mins)

Key Terms for Session 2:

  • The state: Government institutions plus military, police, courts, bureaucracy
  • Civil society: Organizations and relationships outside state and market
  • Dual power: Alternative institutions competing with state authority
  • Prefigurative politics: Organizing in ways that embody desired future
  • Recuperation: System's ability to absorb and neutralize radical challenges
  • Autonomy: Self-governance independent of state control
Participant Research Sharing (15 mins)

Facilitator Script:

"Who researched something to share? Remember, 2 minutes each."

[Keep time strictly, thank each person, note themes on flipchart]

Small Group Exercise: Strategy Mapping (20 mins)

Facilitator Script:

"Form groups of 3-4. Choose a specific issue in Brighton:

  • Homelessness
  • Low wages
  • Climate crisis
  • NHS privatization
  • Police violence

Design a campaign using ALL THREE orientations. What would you do in, against, and beyond the state? How would these strategies support each other?"

[15 mins small group work]

[5 mins report back - each group shares one insight]

Open Discussion (20 mins)

Discussion Prompts:

  1. "Where do you see Your Party on this triangle? Is it purely 'in' or does it engage other orientations?"
  2. "What examples from your own experience combine these strategies?"
  3. "Is working 'in' the state always reformist or can it be revolutionary?"
  4. "How do we avoid the traps of each orientation?"

Facilitator Script for Closing:

"Next week we'll dig into Brighton's radical history - learning how previous generations navigated these strategic questions. If you want to contribute, research a specific event, movement, or figure from Brighton's radical past. We'll build our collective knowledge together."
