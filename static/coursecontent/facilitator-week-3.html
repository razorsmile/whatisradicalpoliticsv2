Week 3 Facilitator Script

This is a script for the third week of the Radical Education course. It is designed to help facilitators run the session effectively.

HOUR 1: Structured Content (60 mins)

Opening (10 mins)

Facilitator Script:

"Tonight we become historians of Brighton's radical past. History isn't neutral - what gets remembered, how it's told, who tells it - these are political questions. We'll explore three historical moments, then build our collective knowledge.

But first - who researched Brighton radical history to share?"

[Note names for hour two]

Part 1: Why Radical History Matters (10 mins)

Facilitator Script:

"Why study radical history locally?

  • Inspiration: Seeing ordinary people achieve extraordinary things
  • Learning: Understanding what worked, what didn't, and why
  • Connection: Realizing we're part of a longer struggle
  • Resources: Discovering tactics, strategies, and models we can adapt

Radical historiography means:

  • Centering marginalized voices
  • Looking for patterns of resistance
  • Questioning official narratives
  • Finding hidden transcripts of struggle

Brighton's tourist-friendly image obscures a rich history of resistance."

Part 2: Three Brighton Moments (30 mins)

Facilitator Script:

1. THE 1934 BRIGHTON UNEMPLOYED WORKERS' MOVEMENT (10 mins)

"During the Great Depression, Brighton had 12,000 unemployed workers. The National Unemployed Workers' Movement, led locally by Bert Santley, organized:

  • Mass marches to the Town Hall
  • Occupations of relief offices
  • Direct action to prevent evictions
  • Mutual aid including communal kitchens

On May Day 1934, 3,000 marched through Brighton demanding work or full maintenance. Police attacked with batons, triggering the 'Battle of Church Street.'

Key lessons:

  • Combined 'against' tactics (confrontation) with 'beyond' (mutual aid)
  • Connected immediate needs to systemic critique
  • Built solidarity across employed/unemployed divide

The movement won increased relief rates and stopped many evictions. Their slogan: 'We Refuse to Starve in Silence.'"

2. THE 1970s BRIGHTON SQUATTING MOVEMENT (10 mins)

"By 1975, Brighton had over 500 squatters while 3,000 properties stood empty. The movement wasn't just about housing - it was prefigurative politics in action.

Key occupied spaces:

  • The Richmond: Squatted hotel becoming housing co-op
  • Wykeham Terrace: Row of mansions turned into commune
  • Multiple empty office blocks converted to homes

They created:

  • Squatters' handbook and legal support network
  • Children's nurseries and food cooperatives
  • The Brighton Voice - radical community newspaper
  • Networks linking housed and unhoused solidarity

The 1977 Criminal Law Act criminalized squatting in residential buildings - direct state response to the movement's success.

Legacy: Several current housing co-ops originated as squats. The model of taking direct action for housing needs continues today."

3. BRIGHTON AGAINST THE POLL TAX (1989-1991) (10 mins)

"The Poll Tax - a flat-rate local tax regardless of income - triggered the largest civil disobedience campaign in modern British history. Brighton was crucial to its defeat.

Brighton's resistance:

  • 30,000 non-payers by 1991 (20% of those liable)
  • Anti-Poll Tax Union met weekly at the Unemployed Centre
  • Mass rallies at the Level
  • Resistance to bailiffs through community phone trees
  • Legal support and court solidarity

March 1990: 3,000 marched through Brighton. When London exploded in the Poll Tax Riots, Brighton activists were there.

Success factors:

  • Unity across class lines
  • Combination of non-payment (against) and mutual support (beyond)
  • Clear demand: abolition not reform
  • Practical solidarity: accompanying people to court

The tax was abolished in 1991. Thatcher resigned. Proof that collective refusal works."

Part 3: Patterns and Methods (10 mins)

Facilitator Script:

"What patterns do we see across these struggles?

Successful tactics:

  • Direct action combined with mutual aid
  • Clear demands linked to systemic critique
  • Practical solidarity not just symbolic support
  • Creating alternative institutions while fighting existing ones

How to research radical history:

  • Oral history: Interview participants while they're alive
  • Alternative archives: Union records, radical newspapers, personal collections
  • Read against the grain: What do hostile sources reveal?
  • Place-based investigation: What happened on your street?
  • Question absences: Whose stories are missing?

Your research matters. Official history won't preserve these stories - we must."

HOUR 2: Open Discussion (60 mins)

Jargon Buster (5 mins)

Key Terms for Session 3:

  • Historiography: The study of how history is written
  • Hidden transcript: Resistance practices invisible to power
  • Collective memory: Shared understanding of the past
  • Counter-narrative: Alternative to dominant historical accounts
  • Oral history: Historical method using interviews and testimony
  • Praxis: Unity of theory and practice
Participant Research Sharing (20 mins)

Facilitator Script:

"Let's hear what you've discovered about Brighton's radical past. As you listen, think about patterns and connections."

[Strict 2-minute time keeping, map connections on timeline]

Collective Mapping Exercise (20 mins)

Facilitator Script:

"Let's build our collective knowledge. On this map of Brighton, let's mark:

  • Sites of historical struggles
  • Current radical spaces
  • Where you live/work/organize
  • Connections between past and present

Come up and add your knowledge. This is our radical geography."

[Participants add to collective map]

"What patterns do you see? Where does resistance cluster? What spaces recur?"

Discussion: Past to Present (15 mins)

Discussion Prompts:

  1. "Which historical tactics could work today? Which couldn't and why?"
  2. "What's different about organizing in 2024 Brighton versus 1934 or 1974?"
  3. "How do we ensure today's struggles become tomorrow's inspiring history?"
  4. "What stories of resistance from your own family or community should be preserved?"

Facilitator Script for Closing:

"Next week, our final session, we'll map working-class counter-power in Brighton today. We'll use methods from Italian Marxist sociology - workers' inquiry and co-research - to identify where collective power exists now.

Optional task: Identify one mutual aid project, self-organized space, or community infrastructure in Brighton. We'll build a resource map together."
